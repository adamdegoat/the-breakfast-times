The Wrong Coat

She walked out of the gallery in a stranger's coat — finer than hers, smelling of cedar and someone else's good decisions — and had to go back for the swap.

Published 8:15 PM SGT·~5 min read

❀

The coat was not hers, which she realised somewhere around the third button, because her coat did not smell like cedar and rain and someone else's good decisions.

Maya stopped on the gallery steps. The January cold went straight through the unfamiliar wool, which was finer than anything she owned, and heavier, and had a paperback shoved in one pocket — dog-eared, she noted, with the page corners turned down by a person who clearly had no respect for books or for the people who loved them. She checked the inside. No name. Just a coat-check tag, number 41, the twin of the one still pinched between her fingers.

She had, in short, walked off with a stranger's life, and left them holding hers.

She went back in. The girl at the coat check was apologetic in the way of someone who had done exactly this before and would do it again. "Number 41 left like ten minutes ago," she said. "But — oh. They came back too." She pointed.

He was standing by the cloakroom in Maya's coat, which on him stopped well above the wrist and strained across the shoulders so that he looked like a man who had lost a bet, holding her tote bag against his chest like he wasn't sure whether it was allowed to breathe. He was looking at her coat-self with an expression she would later describe, to her sister, as caught.

"You read on your phone at parties," he said, by way of hello. "In the corner. I admired the commitment."

"You dog-ear paperbacks," she said, "so I think we both have things to atone for."

He looked down at his own coat on her, and something in his face did a small collapse that she felt in her sternum. "You found Marcus's book," he said. "The — the receipt's in there too, I haven't — it's a long story."

"It's a short book," she said. "I read the first page on the steps. It's good."

"It's his." A beat. "Was. My brother's. I've been carrying it around for a year because I can't decide what to do with a thing someone loved." He said it lightly, the way you say the heaviest things when you've only just met someone and haven't yet earned the right to be heavy. "Anyway. You smell like my coat now. I apologise on its behalf."

"It's a good coat," she admitted.

"It was an expensive mistake I made when I thought being the kind of man who owns a coat like this would fix me." He smiled. "It did not fix me. But it's warm."

They swapped on the steps, which meant a brief, ridiculous choreography of both of them half-undressed in the cold, passing wool back and forth, her arm caught in a sleeve, his laugh fogging the air between them. When she had her own coat back it was warm from him and she hated how much she noticed.

"Okay," he said, doing up her — his — buttons, restored to his proper size and somehow less interesting for it. "Crisis averted. The city is safe. The coats are home."

"The coats are home," she agreed.

Neither of them moved.

The thing about a meet-cute, Maya thought, is that everyone tells you about the meeting and nobody tells you about the part right after, the part on the steps, where the meeting is technically over and you have to decide, like an adult, whether to let it be a story you tell or a thing you do.

"I'm going to say something," he said, "and you should know I'm fully aware it's the coat talking, the borrowed-confidence of a coat that isn't even mine —"

"It's mine," she said.

"It's yours," he agreed, delighted, "which makes it worse, I'm wearing my own coat and borrowing courage from yours, which I think is the most pathetic possible configuration —"

"You're stalling."

"I'm stalling," he admitted. He took a breath. "There's a place around the corner that does terrible coffee and stays open too late, and I have a dead man's excellent paperback I've been too cowardly to finish, and you read the first page and called it good, and I think — I think I'd finish it, if someone read it with me. Tonight. Right now. While we still smell like each other's coats."

Maya looked at him. Behind him the gallery glowed, full of people who had kept their own coats and would go home having met no one.

"You turn the corners down," she said. "Of the pages. It's a dealbreaker."

"I'll stop."

"You won't."

"I won't," he admitted. "But I'll let you go first. Every page. You can fold them or not. I'll read at your speed."

And that — not the coat, not the cedar, not the way he'd held her ordinary tote like it was something on loan from a museum — that was the thing that did it. I'll read at your speed. She had spent a lot of January, a lot of several Januaries, being read at the wrong speed by people who finished her chapters early and got bored.

"Number 41," she said, holding out her hand, "I'm going to need your name. For the coat-check of it all."

"Theo." His hand was cold and certain. "And yours, so I can tell Marcus's book who finally turned up to read it."

"Maya."

"Maya," he said, like he was folding the corner of it down, gently, so he could find his place again later. "Come on. The coffee's bad and the place is warm and we already match."

They went around the corner, in the right coats at last, and the cold didn't stand a chance.
