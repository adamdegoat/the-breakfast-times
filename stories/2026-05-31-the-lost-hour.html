The Lost Hour

The clock outside the chemist's has been eleven minutes fast for thirty years. On the worst morning of a bad month, she decides to fix it — and learns why no one ever has.

Published 8:15 PM SGT·~4 min read

❀

The clock outside the chemist's had been wrong for as long as anyone could remember, and Priya had decided, on the worst morning of a bad month, that she was going to be the one to fix it.

This was not her job. Priya fixed nobody's clocks; she was twenty-six and between things, which is a polite way of saying she had recently been let go from a job she'd hated and missed anyway, the way you miss a tooth. She had walked past the chemist's clock every day for two years — eleven minutes fast, forever, so that the whole street ran slightly ahead of itself — and that morning, with nowhere to be and an hour she didn't know how to spend, she stopped underneath it and thought: somebody should.

So she went in and asked.

The chemist was a man called Mr. Adeyemi, who had owned the shop for thirty years and had, it turned out, a particular relationship with the clock.

"It's eleven minutes fast," Priya said.

"It is," he agreed, not looking up from the prescriptions.

"Do you want me to —" she gestured, vaguely, at the whole concept of correct time. "I'm good with my hands. I could get a ladder."

Now he looked up. He had the face of a man who had spent thirty years being asked for things — for paracetamol, for advice, for the toilet, for the time — and he studied her with an attention that made her feel briefly, pleasantly, like a customer who had asked for the right thing.

"You know why it's fast?" he said.

"Because it's broken?"

"Because in 1994 a woman named Florence ran for that bus" — he nodded out the window at the stop across the road — "every morning, and she was always just late, every morning, by a little. And one day her husband, who could fix anything, set the street clock eleven minutes fast so she'd make it." He smiled at the prescriptions. "She never knew. She just thought she'd finally got the hang of mornings. Made the bus for fifteen years."

Priya looked at the clock through the window. It looked different now, the way a stranger looks different once you know their name.

"Florence is gone now," Mr. Adeyemi said, gently, in the way that means some years ago. "And her husband. And I keep meaning to fix it, and every morning I look at it being wrong, and I think — there's a person out there, right now, making a bus they think they're catching by the skin of their teeth, who's actually catching it with eleven minutes to spare. And they'll never know why. They'll just think they're doing better than they thought." He shrugged. "I haven't had the heart. Is that very silly?"

"No," said Priya, who had not cried in front of a chemist before and was annoyed to find herself starting.

She did not fix the clock.

She bought a packet of plasters she didn't need, because it felt wrong to take up a man's morning and his whole grief and leave with nothing, and Mr. Adeyemi rang it up on a till that was also, she suspected, slightly wrong, and as she left he said, "You're between things."

It wasn't a question. She turned.

"I can always tell," he said. "People who stop to look at the clock. Busy people don't have time to notice time's wrong." He put her plasters in a paper bag, which nobody did anymore. "My Saturday girl's just gone to university. It's only weekends. It's only stacking shelves and being kind to old people who come in lonelier than they are ill." He slid the bag across. "But you'd be eleven minutes early for everything. I'd see to it."

Priya stood in the small warm shop that smelled of paper and menthol and thirty years of being depended on.

"You're offering me a job," she said, "because I noticed your clock was wrong."

"I'm offering you a job," he said, "because you wanted to fix it and then you didn't, once you knew. That's the whole skill, really. Knowing which wrong things to leave alone." He went back to his prescriptions. "Start Saturday. Eight o'clock. Which, by that clock, is ten to eight, so you've no excuse."

She started Saturday.

She was, by the chemist's clock, eleven minutes early for everything for the rest of that year — for the job, and then for the next one it led to, and for the man she'd meet at the bus stop across the road who was always, always just a little late, and whom she would one day, without ever quite explaining why, learn to forgive for it.

And the clock outside the chemist's is still wrong, this morning, right now, by exactly eleven minutes.

Somebody should fix it.

Nobody will.

There's a person out there catching a bus they think they're about to miss — running, certain they've left it too late, their whole chest tight with the particular dread of being not quite enough, not quite on time, not quite ready — and they are going to make it.

They were always going to make it.

They just don't know yet that someone, years ago, loved a person enough to give the whole street eleven extra minutes, and that the love outlived everyone who remembered it, and that it is, even now, quietly holding a bus for a stranger.

Mind the doors. You're earlier than you think.
