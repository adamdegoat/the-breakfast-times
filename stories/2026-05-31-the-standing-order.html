The Standing Order

For three Tuesdays, a corner-shop owner has been pretending not to recognise the man who buys peaches and a scratch card — the best friend who walked out of his life fifteen years ago.

Published 7:44 PM SGT·~10 min read

❀

Dev has been pretending, for three Tuesdays now, that he does not recognise the man who comes in at 8:40 for a tin of peaches and a scratch card.

This is a lie of some ambition, because Dev has known Solomon Adeyemi since they were nine years old. He would know the back of that head in a crowd of ten thousand. He knew it when it had cornrows and when it was shaved to the wood and now, apparently, when it has gone grey at the temples like someone dusted it with flour. But Sol comes to the counter and says, "Just these, thanks," in the careful voice you use for strangers, and Dev says, "One twenty on the card, two pound ten the lot," in the same voice, and they hand the world back and forth between them like two men who have never once shared a tent in Dev's back garden during the summer it never stopped raining.

The shop is called Murthy & Son, which is a small joke that has stopped being funny. The Murthy was Dev's father. Dev is the Son. There is no longer anyone for the and to connect, so it just sits there on the sign in faded gold, conjunction-ing nothing.

It is the kind of shop that the high street keeps trying to kill. Bread, milk, the newspapers fewer people read, the lottery, the bananas that go spotty by Thursday. Dev knows the regulars by their orders rather than their names: Forty Benson, who quit smoking on the first of every month and resumed on the third; Two Pints Of Blue, a nurse coming off nights with violet shadows under her eyes; The Lady Who Counts Out The Coppers, who is teaching her granddaughter the names of the coins. He likes them. He would not say this out loud, but he likes them the way you like a song you've heard so many times it has become a kind of weather.

And then, three Tuesdays ago, the bell over the door went and the weather changed.

The thing between Dev and Sol — the thing they are now elaborately not mentioning — happened fifteen years ago and it was, Dev has decided in the long account-keeping of the small hours, nobody's fault and entirely Sol's fault, depending on the night.

His father was dying. It took a year, the way it does, in a series of false summits. And Sol, who was supposed to be Dev's best man in eleven weeks, who had a speech and a suit on hold, got a job. A good one. In Aberdeen, which from their corner of London might as well have been the moon. He took it. He left in the middle of the bad year, and he was not there for the last false summit that turned out to be the real one, and he was not there at the back of the crematorium, and the speech was given by Dev's cousin Anand, who called him Devraj the whole time because he didn't know that nobody had called him that since primary school.

Sol sent a card. Dev never opened it. It is, in fact, still in the drawer under the till, and he is aware of it the way you are aware of a tooth.

So that is the standing order between them. Peaches, a scratch card, and fifteen years of unopened post.

On the fourth Tuesday it rains, properly, the kind that bounces.

Sol comes in shaking water off a golf umbrella that has seen action, and he is reaching for the peaches when Dev hears his own voice do something he did not authorise.

"We've got the ones in juice now," Dev says. "Not just syrup. If you — some people prefer the juice."

It is the longest sentence either of them has said to the other in fifteen years and it is about tinned fruit.

Sol goes still. Then, without turning round, in a voice that has lost some of its carefulness: "Your dad only ever stocked syrup. Said juice was for people who'd lost their nerve."

The shop holds its breath. The fridge hums. Somewhere on the radio a man is reading the shipping forecast like a lullaby for a country that mostly lives inland.

"He said that about a lot of things," Dev says.

"He said it about me," Sol says, and now he does turn round, and there it is — the whole face, not the back of the head, the face with fifteen extra years stitched into it but unmistakably the boy who taught Dev to ride a bike by simply refusing to let go of the saddle and then lying about it afterwards. "He said, that boy's got one foot out the door his whole life." A breath. "He wasn't wrong, was he."

Dev should say something kind. He has imagined this conversation enough times to have a kind thing ready. Instead what comes out, dry as a cracker, is: "He also said decaf was a sin against the Holy Ghost, so."

And Sol laughs. It cracks out of him like something breaking, but breaking open, and it is the exact laugh from the tent in the rain, and Dev feels fifteen years do something complicated in his chest.

"Two pound ten," Dev says, because he has to say something. "The lot."

Sol pays in coins. Counts them out on the counter, slow, like The Lady Who Counts The Coppers, like he's teaching someone the names. "Keep the card," he says, pushing the scratch card back. "I never win."

"Nobody wins," Dev says. "That's the business model."

After that the careful voice is gone but neither of them quite knows what to put where it was.

So they put in small things. Sol mentions, the next week, that he's back because his mum's not well, that he's been up in Scotland all this time, that there was a marriage and then there wasn't. Dev offers, in exchange, that the shop is dying, that the supermarket two streets over does a meal deal that has personally killed his sandwich trade, that he hasn't decided yet whether he's the captain who goes down with the ship or the rat who's already eyeing the gangplank. They trade these the way they once traded football stickers — got, got, need, got, need.

Sol starts coming in not just at 8:40 but sometimes at six, when Dev's bringing in the papers, and he holds the door with his bad umbrella while Dev hauls the bundles, and he doesn't ask if Dev wants help, he just helps, which Dev's father also used to say was the only kind of help worth anything.

What neither of them says is the card. The card in the drawer. It sits there through April being a tooth.

It is The Lady Who Counts The Coppers who, without knowing it, opens the drawer.

She comes in one morning with her granddaughter, who is six and serious and sounding out the words on the lottery sign — MUST. BE. EIGHTEEN. OR. OVER — with the ferocity of someone defusing a bomb. The grandmother is short by forty pence for her shopping and is putting back a tin of soup with great dignity, and before Dev can wave it off, Sol — who is in the corner pretending to choose a birthday card for his mum — puts the forty pence on the counter.

"Go on," he says. "I've had a win."

"You've never had a win in your life," Dev says.

"I've had recent wins," Sol says, looking at him, and the look is so direct that Dev has to busy himself with the bag.

When they've gone, the bell jangling, the little girl still reading the world out loud as she goes — THANK. YOU. COME. AGAIN — Sol says, quietly, to the rack of birthday cards, "I wrote you one of these. Once."

"I know," Dev says.

"You never said anything back."

"I never opened it."

He hadn't meant to say it. It lands in the shop like a dropped jar. Sol turns. And Dev, fifty years old next year and tired of the tooth, opens the drawer under the till and takes it out — the envelope soft now, furred at the corners from fifteen years of being not-quite-thrown-away, Dev on the front in Sol's terrible left-handed scrawl.

"You kept it," Sol says.

"I kept it shut," Dev says. "There's a difference. I was very committed to the difference."

They look at the envelope on the counter between the chewing gum and the charity box.

"Do you want to," Sol starts.

"No," Dev says. Then: "Read it to me."

"What?"

"I can't —" Dev gestures, uselessly, at the whole of himself. "I've spent fifteen years not reading it. I don't trust my own eyes with it. Read it to me. Out loud. Like she does." He nods at the door the little girl has just gone through, sounding out the world so it can't sneak up on her.

So Sol picks up the envelope, and his hands are not steady, and he tears it along the top the way you're not supposed to, and he unfolds a card with a cartoon dog on the front holding a single wilted flower, because in 2011 Sol's emotional vocabulary was apparently a sad dog, and he reads.

It is not a good card. This is the thing that undoes Dev completely.

It is not eloquent. Sol was never eloquent; that was always Dev's department. It says he's sorry. It says he was a coward and Aberdeen was just the nearest place to be a coward in. It says your dad was more of a dad to me than mine ever managed and I couldn't watch it, Dev, I couldn't watch the second one go, and that's not an excuse it's just the true thing. It says he'll understand if Dev never wants to see him again. It says — and here Sol's voice, reading it, finally gives out, so that he has to push the last bit through a closing throat — but you should know I'd have come back the second you called. I left the door open my whole life, mate. I just always thought I'd be leaving through it. I never once thought it might be for letting someone back in.

The radio plays. The fridge hums. The charity box has eleven pence in it.

"That's it," Sol says. "That's the whole — there's a P.S. but it's just about the football."

"Read the football," Dev says, because he cannot speak yet.

"P.S.," Sol reads, wrecked, smiling, "we're going to win the league this year, I can feel it." A beat. "We came eighth."

And Dev laughs, the same crack-it-open laugh, and then he is not laughing, and Sol comes round the counter — round to the Son's side, the side no customer is allowed — and the two of them stand there in the small kingdom of bread and milk and spotty bananas and hold onto each other while the bell over the door stays mercifully, gloriously still.

It does not fix everything, because nothing fixes everything. The shop is still dying. Sol's mum is still not well. Fifteen years do not unspool in a morning over a cartoon dog.

But here is what happens, small and true: Sol starts doing the early shift on Saturdays, unpaid, undiscussed, simply there at six with two coffees from the machine he's quietly fixed. He has a head for numbers Dev never had, and one wet Tuesday he looks up from the ledger and says, "You're not the captain or the rat. You're the bloke who keeps the lighthouse on. People need the light on, Dev. We just have to make the light cheaper to run," and then he says something about wholesale and standing orders and a delivery round for the housebound that Dev only half follows but which, by August, has the bell over the door going more than it has in years.

And the sign still says Murthy & Son, and the and still connects nothing, technically.

But on the first Saturday in September, Dev comes in at six and finds that Sol has been up a ladder. Under MURTHY & SON, in gold paint that doesn't quite match because the shop has never once done anything that quite matches, he has added a single word.

&.

Just the ampersand. Hanging there. An and with nothing after it yet, an open door, a sentence the two of them have decided, at last, to leave unfinished on purpose.

"It's grammatically a disaster," Dev says, looking up at it, his throat doing the thing again.

"Yeah, well," Sol says, climbing down, wiping gold off his hands onto his jeans, exactly the way he did when they were nine and the whole long unwritten story of them was still ahead. "Some of us lost our nerve for the juice. Some of us came back for the syrup."

He picks a tin of peaches off the shelf, the syrup ones, his father's ones, and sets it on the counter between them.

"Two pound ten," Dev says. "The lot."

"Put it on my standing order," says Sol, and the bell over the door, when the next customer comes, sounds like nothing so much as someone, somewhere, finally being let in.
