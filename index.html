Cold Water

Roped into fake-dating her best friend's brother for a family lake weekend, she swears it's a favor — until a cold morning swim and one overheard sentence make pretending impossible.

Published 5:14 PM SGT·~13 min read

❀

The citronella candle had burned to a waxy nub and started to smell like singed hair, and Declan's knee was two inches from mine on the dock, and his sister was watching us from the porch like she was grading a test she'd written herself.

I smiled at her. She beamed back, raised her wine glass, and disappeared inside.

"She's gone," I said.

"She'll be back." Declan stretched his arms overhead, and the dock shifted under the weight of him, that slight give of old wood. "She did this at Christmas too. Just when you think she's given up, she reappears behind a houseplant."

"There are no houseplants out here."

"There's that big pine."

He wasn't wrong. I'd known Priya for eleven years — since our first week of university, when she'd commandeered my laundry cart and never fully given it back — and she approached matchmaking the way she approached client acquisitions: with research, patience, and absolutely no shame about her methods. She'd been trying to get me and her brother in the same room for two years. Then, three weeks ago, she'd tried a new angle.

My whole family is doing a lake house rental for the long weekend. Six days. Parents, aunts, uncles, cousins. They keep asking if I'm seeing anyone and I'm so tired, Lena. Will you just — come with me and be my, not boyfriend, I don't need that, just, Declan's date? He hates these things and you hate these things and you both need to be saved and also maybe I want you to fall in love with him a little bit.

She'd actually written that last part. I'd screenshotted it so I'd have proof, later, that none of this was my idea.

Declan hadn't been much more enthusiastic. I'd gotten a text at eleven the night before we left: Priya tells me we're doing a thing. I can work with a thing if you can. Also she said not to mention she said that.

I'd met him six times in eleven years. He was a structural engineer who lived in Vancouver and came home to Ottawa twice a year with the careful efficiency of a man fulfilling a contract. He was thirty-four. I was thirty-one. He was the kind of quiet that people misread as cold, and I was the kind of loud that people misread as confident, and I had no business agreeing to this except that I was twenty-two months out of a four-year relationship and my mother had started sending me articles with titles like Embracing a Full Life on Your Own Terms and a pointed absence of commentary.

I was thirty-one. I was fine. I just wanted a weekend where nobody asked me about it.

The lake house was on Sharbot Lake, three hours east of the city, and it was the kind of beautiful that felt almost rude — white birches against green water, a rope swing nobody over forty was going to use, a screen door that slapped every time someone came through it. Priya's mother had greeted me with a hug that lasted long enough to feel like a decision.

"Finally," she'd said, which was a lot of subtext for one word.

Declan had arrived twenty minutes after me, still in work clothes, a duffel over one shoulder, and when he saw me in the kitchen he'd done something with his face — a quick recalibration, like a compass finding north. He'd crossed to me and hugged me with the practiced ease of a man who'd been briefed, and I'd let him because I'd also been briefed, and it had been fine. Ordinary. A hug.

Then his mother said, "Isn't she lovely, Dec?" in a voice that held ten years of wanting grandchildren, and I felt him breathe out against my hair — the specific, exhausted exhale of a man who loved his family very much and also needed them to take one step back — and something in me recognized that exhale with an accuracy that was frankly inconvenient.

We'd settled our terms that night, on a walk down to the water while Priya organized dinner with the authority of a general.

"We're dating, but we're private," Declan said. "Nobody needs details. We met through Priya, we've been seeing each other a few months. That's it."

"And we're not —" I started.

"No."

"I wasn't going to —"

"I know what you were going to say."

"You don't."

"You were going to say we're not letting this get weird," he said. "Which is the thing people say right before things get weird."

He was infuriating. I'd forgotten that part. The two times I'd spent more than an hour with him — Priya's thirtieth, her housewarming — he'd had the quality of a person three sentences ahead of the conversation. It had annoyed me both times. I'd thought about it afterward more than I should have.

"Fine," I said. "No terms. Let's just see what happens."

He looked at the water for a moment. "Okay," he said, and I couldn't tell if he was relieved or something else.

By day three, we'd found a rhythm easier than it had any right to be.

He made coffee before anyone else was up, and he made it correctly — strong, no sugar — and he'd started leaving a mug on the counter for me without being asked, which I chose not to examine. I'd started saving him a seat at dinner, sliding my bag off the chair beside me when I heard his step on the porch, which he never mentioned. His cousin Rohan pulled me aside on the second day and said, with the candor of a twelve-year-old, "He talks more when you're around," and I said, "That's nice," and then spent forty-five minutes alone in a canoe thinking about it.

The canoe had become my hiding place. There was something about being thirty feet offshore — nobody able to reach me except by swimming or shouting — that restored something the family noise, warm as it was, kept wearing down. I'd grown up an only child. I loved Priya's family the way I loved a city: in short, bright doses, followed by a quiet room.

Declan found me out there on the fourth morning — the good one, the one I kept going back to in the weeks after.

I was reading, or trying to. The water kept catching the light and throwing it around in a way that made concentration impossible, and I heard a splash off the dock and looked up to see him in the lake, swimming toward me with the unhurried confidence of a man raised on water. He surfaced beside the canoe and rested his arms on the gunwale and blinked the lake out of his eyes, his hair flat against his head, and I thought: that's the version of him nobody performs for.

"It's cold," I said.

"It's seven in the morning."

"That's not an answer."

"It's always cold at seven in the morning." Like this was obvious. "That's the point."

I set my book down. "The point of cold water is that it's cold?"

"The point is you can't be half in it." He tipped his head back, let the water take some of his weight. "You're either in or you're not. There's no sitting on the edge, wondering."

I was quiet for a moment. Somewhere in the reeds, a loon let out its long, lonesome trill — the one that sounds like a question nobody's answered.

"I don't swim in the mornings," I said.

"I know. I've noticed." He wasn't looking at me now. The sun was still low enough that the water was all amber and shadow. "You notice things," he said, "but you don't usually let them change what you do."

"That's a confident read of a woman you've spent four days with."

"I've been watching you for longer than four days."

I didn't say anything. The canoe rocked in the wake of a boat far out on the main channel.

"At Priya's birthday," he said, not as a question. "And the housewarming. And the time she dragged us both to that gallery opening we both hated, three years ago." He looked up at me then. "You stood next to the same painting for forty minutes because the crowd between you and the door was too thick and you didn't want to push through. You found the one space that was bearable and you stayed in it."

The accuracy of it landed somewhere under my ribs and sat there.

"And now you come out here," he said. "Same principle."

"Are you analyzing me?"

"I'm telling you what I've noticed." A pause. "Is that all right?"

I looked down at him in the water — at the angle of his jaw in the early light, at the quality of his waiting. He waited like a man with nothing to lose. I wondered if that was true, or just very well maintained.

"Yes," I said. "It's all right."

He didn't smile at me, exactly. More like he let a thought through by accident. Then he pushed off the canoe and swam back to the dock, and I watched him go, and the loon called again from the reeds, and I sat out there another forty minutes before I trusted myself to come in.

Priya found me on the porch that night. She had the look she always got before she said something she'd rehearsed — a practiced casualness, like she'd pre-loaded the sentence.

"You should know," she said, "I wasn't entirely selfless in arranging this."

"I know."

"I've always thought you two —"

"Priya."

"Just say you'll think about it."

"Think about what? We have a deal."

She looked at me the way she'd been looking at me since university — the look that meant I'm not going to say it, but I'm also not not going to say it.

"The deal," she said, "looks a lot different from outside than I think it does from inside."

After she went to bed I sat there a long time, moths knocking against the porch light, the screen door slapping now and then in the warm breeze off the water. I thought about four years with Marcus, who'd been good and smart, and whom I'd loved in the steady, careful way you love a thing you're also a little afraid to need. We'd ended it mutually, which somehow made it worse — no villain, no wound with a clean edge, just the joint admission that we'd both been waiting for the other one to feel more. We'd been each other's almost.

I didn't want to be anyone's almost again. That was the thing I couldn't say out loud without sounding like a headline.

And the thing I also couldn't say: the canoe that morning had done something four days of careful performing had not.

I was in the kitchen the next afternoon when I heard Declan's voice from the next room — not the words, just the cadence of him, that pitch he used when he was choosing words carefully. Then his mother. Then her cadence, which I knew by now too: warm, hopeful, leaning forward.

I wasn't trying to listen. The house was small.

"She seems different from the others," his mother said.

"Ma."

"I'm only saying. You always pick women who are almost ready to leave. I've never known if that's strategy or just bad luck."

A long pause. "I'm aware," Declan said, quietly.

"Is she staying?"

"I don't know yet."

"Do you want her to?"

The silence stretched long enough that I stopped pretending I wasn't listening and stood very still at the kitchen window, looking at the water, at the late light going flat and gold.

"I don't know," he said, "if what I want is relevant."

I set my glass down and walked out to the back porch and stayed at the railing until the light was gone.

You always pick women who are almost ready to leave. I didn't know whether that was something to run from or something to run toward, and the not-knowing was a feeling I recognized — the same one I'd had in the canoe, the same one I'd carried for four years, the one where I found the safe corner and stayed in it rather than push through the crowd.

So I went to find him.

He was at the end of the dock, feet over the water. The screen door slapped behind me and I didn't look back. The boards gave under me — that flex of old wood — and I sat down closer than we'd been on the first night, and for a while neither of us said anything.

"I heard some of that," I said. "What your mother said."

He nodded once. He'd been expecting it, maybe. He was always ahead of me.

"She's not wrong," he said. "About the pattern."

"She might be wrong about me."

"Might."

I turned so I was looking right at him. He kept his eyes on the water, but his jaw had set in the way I'd learned meant he was paying more attention than he was letting on.

"I'm not almost ready to leave," I said.

"You don't know that. It's been six days."

"I know I've thought more clearly in that canoe this week than I have in two years. I know you make my coffee right without asking. I know that every time you laugh at something I said, I feel like I won a thing I'd been trying to win." I stopped. Started again. "I am very afraid of being someone's convenient almost. I was that, for a long time. And I am also —" I pressed my palm flat to the boards between us. "I'm tired of finding the one safe corner and staying in it for the rest of my life. You said that. You were right."

Now he looked at me.

"This started as a thing," I said. "Priya's thing. I know that."

"Yes."

"Is it still only that?"

The question sat between us in the dark. Out on the water the loons were going back and forth — that wild, asking sound — and up at the house someone laughed and the screen door slapped. He reached over and laid his hand over mine on the dock, and the warmth of it was so specific, so quietly certain, that everything restless in me went still.

"No," he said. "It stopped being only that by the second morning."

I turned my hand over under his.

"The canoe," I said.

"Before the canoe," he said. "When you saved my seat at dinner without saying anything. I almost said something then."

"Why didn't you?"

He was quiet, and I felt the old wound move through the pause — the echo of his mother's voice, women who are almost ready to leave, the way a person learns to outwait his own wanting.

"Because I didn't know if you'd stay," he said.

"I'm on a dock in cottage country at eleven at night," I said. "I came and found you. I'm here."

He looked at me in the dark with that recalibrating expression — the compass again, finding north — and then he curved his hand along the line of my jaw and closed the last of the distance himself, and the loons called once more across the black water, that long wild note, and I stopped cataloguing the moment and let myself be all the way in it.

We drove back to the city together. Priya rode with her parents — graciously, and with a great deal of eyebrow.

She texted me from their car: So.

I looked at Declan. He was watching the highway, one hand easy on the wheel, and he glanced over when he felt me looking — that quick, dark look, the one he'd been giving me all morning over coffee while his family pretended not to notice and failed completely.

I texted back: So.

Her next message was a string of characters I chose to read as dignified.

The sun had reached the late angle that makes an Ontario highway look like something worth staying for — the pines going dark green, the sky doing things with gold that no single word ever quite catches — and Declan reached over without looking and laid his hand over mine on the console, the same way he had on the dock, with that quiet certainty that had undone me the first time and was undoing me again. I turned to the window so he wouldn't see me smiling like someone who'd finally stopped looking for the edge of the crowd and walked straight through it.

The road kept going. I didn't mind.
